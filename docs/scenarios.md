## Scenario 1: The Status Quo

**Purpose:** Understand your baseline trajectory

**Setup:**
```
Current Age: [Your age]
Current Savings: [Your actual amount]
Monthly Contribution: [Your actual amount]
Stop Contributing: [At retirement age]
All other fields: Your realistic assumptions
```

**What to record:**
- Coast FIRE age (when you could stop saving)
- Portfolio at retirement
- Annual retirement income
- Years of saving required

**Example Results:**
```
Current age: 35
Coast FIRE age: 45 (10 years away)
Portfolio at 65: $1,200,000
Annual income: $48,000
Total to contribute: $216,000
```

**Questions to ask:**
- Am I satisfied with this timeline?
- Is the retirement income sufficient?
- Can I maintain these contributions?
- What happens if I change jobs?

## Scenario 3: The Sabbatical

**Purpose:** Quantify the cost of taking a career break

**Setup:**
```
Stop Contributing: [Start of break, e.g., age 40]
Monthly Contribution: $0 (simulating the break)

```

**What to record:**
- Portfolio at retirement with break
- Difference vs continuous saving
- Cost per year of break

**Example Results:**
```

Option B - Stop at 40 (5-year break before planned stop at 45):
Portfolio at 65: $1,050,000

Cost of break: $150,000
Cost per year: $30,000
```

**Advanced calculation:**
If you resume saving after the break, test a third scenario:
```

Stop at 40, resume at 45 with monthly contributions

```

**Questions to ask:**
- Is the life experience worth $150,000?
- Could I work part-time during the break to offset?
- Would I regret not taking the break?
- Can I make it up later?

## Scenario 4: The Aggressive Push

**Purpose:** Find your fastest path to Coast FIRE

**Setup:**
```
Monthly Contribution: [2x your current amount]
Try: [1.5x], [2x], [2.5x], [3x] your current contribution
Stop Contributing: [Varies - when Coast FIRE is reached]
```

**What to record for each level:**
- Coast FIRE age
- Total amount to contribute
- Years of aggressive saving
- Portfolio at retirement

**Example Results:**
```
Current: $1,500/month
- Coast FIRE at: 45
- Years saving: 10 years
- Total contributed: $180,000

Double ($3,000/month):
- Coast FIRE at: 40
- Years saving: 5 years
- Total contributed: $180,000

Triple ($4,500/month):
- Coast FIRE at: 38
- Years saving: 3 years
- Total contributed: $162,000
```

**The surprise:** Sometimes saving more aggressively requires LESS total contribution because you reach Coast FIRE sooner!

**Questions to ask:**
- Can I realistically save this much?
- Is 3 years of intense saving worth 7 years earlier freedom?
- What would I need to cut?
- Could I increase income instead of cutting expenses?

## Scenario 5: The Lean FIRE

**Purpose:** See how reduced expenses change your timeline

**Setup:**
```
Annual Expenses: [75% of current estimate]
Try: 90%, 80%, 75%, 70% of current expenses
All other fields: Same as Scenario 1
```

**What to record:**
- Coast FIRE target (how much you need today)
- Coast FIRE age
- Portfolio required at retirement
- How close you are to Coast FIRE

**Example Results:**
```
Original ($40,000/year expenses):
- Coast FIRE target: $285,000
- Current savings: $150,000
- Need: $135,000 more

Lean ($30,000/year expenses - 25% reduction):
- Coast FIRE target: $214,000
- Current savings: $150,000
- Need: $64,000 more (already 70% there!)
```

**The power of lean:**
Reducing expenses by 25% cuts your Coast FIRE target by ~25%!

**Questions to ask:**
- What could I cut without reducing happiness?
- Which expenses are worth keeping?
- Could I live this way long-term?
- Would I feel deprived?

### Investment Return Scenarios

Test three return assumptions to understand sensitivity.

**Conservative (5% real return):**
```
Expected Return: 7.5%
Inflation: 2.5%
Real return: 5%
```

**Moderate (7% total return):**
```
Expected Return: 7%
Inflation: 2.5%
Real return: 4.37%
```

**Aggressive (9% total return):**
```
Expected Return: 9%
Inflation: 2.5%
Real return: 6.34%
```

**Example comparison:**
```
Starting with $150,000, saving $1,500/month for 10 years:

Conservative (5% real):
- Portfolio at retirement: $950,000

Moderate (7% total):
- Portfolio at retirement: $1,200,000

Aggressive (9% total):
- Portfolio at retirement: $1,520,000

Range: $570,000 difference!
```

**Key insight:** Returns matter enormously over 30+ years!

**Decision rule:**
- Plan with moderate assumptions (7%)
- Hope for aggressive results (9%)
- Prepare for conservative reality (5%)
- If conservative case works, you're golden!

### Inflation Scenarios

Test how different inflation rates affect your plan.

**Low (2% inflation):**
```
Expected Return: 7%
Inflation: 2%
Real return: 4.9%
```

**Moderate (2.5% inflation):**
```
Expected Return: 7%
Inflation: 2.5%
Real return: 4.37%
```

**High (4% inflation):**
```
Expected Return: 7%
Inflation: 4%
Real return: 2.88%
```

**Example comparison:**
```
Starting with $200,000, no contributions, 30 years:

Low (2% inflation):
- Real portfolio: $1,007,000

Moderate (2.5% inflation):
- Real portfolio: $847,000

High (4% inflation):
- Real portfolio: $476,000

Range: $531,000 difference!
```

**Key insight:** High inflation is devastating to long-term plans!

**Mitigation strategies:**
- Invest in stocks (inflation hedge)
- Plan with 2.5-3% inflation
- Monitor actual inflation annually
- Adjust contributions if needed

### Withdrawal Rate Scenarios

Test different safe withdrawal rates to understand income flexibility.

**Ultra-Safe (3.5%):**
```
Safe Withdrawal Rate: 3.5%
Historical success: 98%
Best for: 40+ year retirements
```

**Standard (4%):**
```
Safe Withdrawal Rate: 4%
Historical success: 95%
Best for: 30-35 year retirements
```

**Aggressive (5%):**
```
Safe Withdrawal Rate: 5%
Historical success: 85%
Best for: Risk-tolerant, flexible spenders
```

**Example comparison:**
```
Portfolio at retirement: $1,000,000

Ultra-Safe (3.5%):
- Annual income: $35,000
- Very sustainable

Standard (4%):
- Annual income: $40,000
- Historically sustainable

Aggressive (5%):
- Annual income: $50,000
- Requires flexibility
```

**Decision factors:**
- **Use 3.5% if:** Long retirement (40+ years), want maximum safety
- **Use 4% if:** Standard retirement (30-35 years), comfortable with historical data
- **Use 5% if:** Flexible spending, can reduce in bad years, other income sources

## ðŸ’¡ Pro Scenario Planning Tips

### Tip 1: The "Rule of Three"
Always test at least 3 scenarios:
1. Best case
2. Expected case  
3. Worst case

If worst case is acceptable, proceed confidently!

### Tip 2: The "One Variable at a Time" Rule
When testing scenarios, change ONE variable at a time to understand its impact.

Bad approach:
```
Change return from 7% to 8%
AND change expenses from $40K to $35K
AND change withdrawal rate from 4% to 5%
```
You won't know which change mattered!

Good approach:
```
Scenario 1: Just change return to 8%
Scenario 2: Just change expenses to $35K
Scenario 3: Just change withdrawal to 5%
```

### Tip 3: The "Life Happens" Buffer
Add 10-20% to everything:
- Expenses: Plan for 10% more than you think
- Timeline: Assume you'll work 1-2 years longer
- Savings: Target 10% more than calculator says

Life never goes exactly to plan!

### Tip 4: The Annual Adjustment
Run all scenarios again every year:
- Markets change
- Your goals evolve
- Life circumstances shift
- Update and iterate!
